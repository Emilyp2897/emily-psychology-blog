Four tools.

## Tool 1: The Pre-Match Focus Window

**When to use:** the 30-60 minutes before throw-in.

**How to do it:**

Build a routine with these phases:

1. **Phase 1 (T-60 to T-45):** physical prep. Strapping, warm-up start, light touches.
2. **Phase 2 (T-45 to T-25):** social phase. Connect with teammates, brief tactical reminder, light chat. The team's energy comes together.
3. **Phase 3 (T-25 to T-10):** individual focus. Quieter. Imagery if you use it. Pre-match cue word. Whatever gets your arousal to the right level.
4. **Phase 4 (T-10 to throw-in):** ready state. Boots double-checked, gum shield in, eyes up.

Within this, know:
- Do you need to be hyped or calm at throw-in? Most players don't actually know.
- What music (if any) works?
- Who do you avoid in the dressing room (the energy-drain) and who do you stand near (the energy-lift)?

**Why it works:** routines reduce cognitive load. Your brain doesn't have to decide what to do; it just runs the routine. This frees attention for what matters once the match starts. Pre-performance routine research (Cotterill, 2010) shows consistent routines reliably improve focus and reduce anxiety.

## Tool 2: External Focus Cues

**When to use:** any time your attention drifts inward (worrying, watching yourself play, ruminating).

**How to do it:**

Have a short list of EXTERNAL focus cues. Things outside you.

- The ball. "Where is it now?"
- Your player. "Where is she?"
- The space. "Where's the gap?"
- The shape. "Where do I need to be?"

When you notice your attention pulling inward ("what do I do, what if I miss"), pivot to one external cue. Say it in your head if needed.

**Why it works:** internal focus on technique disrupts automatic execution (Beilock & Carr, 2001). External focus on the task keeps you in the play. The shift takes one second once you've practised it.

## Tool 3: The 60-Second Re-Settle

**When to use:** mid-match flow loss. The match shifted, you got pulled out of rhythm, you need to find your level again.

**How to do it:**

When the ball goes dead (kickout, side-line, score):

1. One slow exhale. Drop the shoulders.
2. Look around. Find one teammate. Make eye contact.
3. Pick one process action for the next 60 seconds. "I'm going to be the first to a 50-50." "I'm calling my marker."
4. Go.

Total time: about 8 seconds. Then 60 seconds of focused action.

**Why it works:** flow loss in-game is usually accompanied by mental scatter (worrying about the next thing, ruminating on the last thing). The 60-second re-settle narrows attention back to a single, achievable action. Once you've done it successfully, momentum can return.

## Tool 4: The Match Compartments

**When to use:** in long matches where you tend to lose focus in stretches.

**How to do it:** mentally divide the match into compartments.

- 0-15 minutes: settle.
- 15-30 minutes: build.
- Half-time.
- 35-50 minutes: push.
- 50-end: hold or close.

In each compartment, have a different process focus. "Settle" might be about reads and positioning. "Push" might be about tempo and assertiveness.

This stops your brain from carrying the whole 60 minutes at once. Long-match anxiety is often the result of trying to mentally hold the entire game. Compartments let you focus on the current chunk.

**Why it works:** chunking is a well-documented cognitive strategy. Long, undifferentiated tasks drain mental energy faster than the same task broken into compartments with clear transitions.

## Putting it together

**Before the match:**
- Pre-Match Focus Window (the 4-phase routine).

**During the match:**
- External focus cues to interrupt internal drift.
- 60-second re-settle when flow is lost.
- Match compartments to chunk the time.

If you only learn one of these, learn external focus cues. They work in seconds and you can deploy them anywhere.
