This is the practical companion to the Month 1 posts on pressure. If you've read [How to bring your focus back when your mind starts to drift](/content-hub/training-the-mind/attention-control-theory/) or [How Pressure Shows in Ladies Football](/content-hub/training-the-mind/how-pressure-shows-in-ladies-football/), this is where you actually do something about it.

Three tools. Use one. Use all three. Mix them with your own. The point is that something is in your back pocket for when the pressure starts to rise.

## Tool 1: The 4-7-8 breath

**When to use:** in the warm-up, before a free, at half time, any moment you feel your heart rate climb past where it needs to be.

**How to do it:**
1. Breathe in through your nose for 4 seconds.
2. Hold the breath for 7 seconds.
3. Breathe out slowly through your mouth for 8 seconds.
4. Repeat 2 to 4 times.

**Why it works:** the slow exhale activates the parasympathetic nervous system (the calming side). It physically lowers heart rate within about a minute. Practitioners use this in clinical anxiety care because the evidence is consistent. You don't need to believe in it for it to work.

## Tool 2: A 3-step pre-match routine

**When to use:** in the 10 minutes before the throw-in, or before a specific moment (free, sideline, kickout).

**How to do it:**
1. **One physical anchor.** Tie your boots. Adjust your gum shield. Roll your shoulders. Something the same every time. This tells your body "we're starting".
2. **One breath.** Slow in, slow out. Just one. Don't make it complicated.
3. **One cue word.** Pick one. "Sharp." "Present." "Calm." "Trust." Say it in your head. Same word every time.

**Why it works:** routines reduce decision-making in high-pressure moments. Your brain has fewer choices to weigh, which frees up energy for what matters (reading the play). Research on Individual Zones of Optimal Functioning (IZOF) shows that consistency in routine is more important than the specific routine itself.

## Tool 3: Cue words for during the game

**When to use:** mid-game when your mind starts to wander or you feel yourself tightening up.

**How to do it:** pick one word per situation, in advance, and rehearse it in training.

- For drift: **"present"** or **"here"**
- For overthinking: **"trust"** or **"natural"**
- For after a mistake: **"next ball"** or **"gone"**
- For tightness: **"loose"** or **"breathe"**
- For low energy: **"sharp"** or **"go"**

**Why it works:** a one-word cue gives your conscious mind a job that doesn't interfere with execution. It pulls you out of the over-analysis spiral that Choking Under Pressure research (Beilock & Carr, 2001) identifies as the main cause of skill breakdown.

## A note on what cue words AREN'T

They're not affirmations. Saying "I'm the best" when you don't believe it makes things worse. Cue words are for re-focusing your attention, not for talking yourself into a feeling. Keep them functional, not motivational.

## Putting it together

Build a 30-second pre-match routine this week. Three parts: anchor → breath → cue word. Use the same one in training. By the time it matters, it'll feel automatic.
