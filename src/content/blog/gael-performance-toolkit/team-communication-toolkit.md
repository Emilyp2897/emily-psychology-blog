Four tools.

## Tool 1: The Specific Recognition Script

**When to use:** when you notice a teammate did something well that wasn't obvious to everyone.

**How to do it:**

The formula: **specific action + impact**.

Examples:
- "Your tackle in the 18th minute changed the game. We needed that."
- "I noticed you settled down beside her when she got dropped. That was a good move."
- "Your communication in the second half on the kickouts. I heard every call."

NOT:
- "Great game"
- "Well done"
- "You played really well"

**Why it works:** general praise feels nice but doesn't build confidence. Specific praise is evidence. The recipient can use it. Bandura's self-efficacy research shows that mastery experiences are the strongest source of confidence, and being told you did a specific thing well is part of how you encode them.

## Tool 2: The Assertive Request

**When to use:** when something is bothering you and you need to address it with a teammate or coach.

**How to do it:** use **DESC**:

- **Describe** the specific behaviour. "When you raised your voice at me in the drill yesterday..."
- **Express** the impact in plain terms. "...it made me freeze up for the rest of the session."
- **Specify** what you want different. "I'd appreciate if you could pull me aside instead."
- **Consequence** (positive). "That would help me play freer in training."

This works for coach conversations, captain conversations, teammate conversations. The framework keeps you grounded when emotions are high.

**Why it works:** comes from assertiveness training research. Avoids the two failure modes most athletes default to: silence (the issue grows) or aggression (the relationship gets damaged).

## Tool 3: The Conflict Repair Script

**When to use:** after a disagreement with a teammate or coach where things got tense.

**How to do it:** find them. Say one thing. Three options:

- **Acknowledge:** "I think I came in too hot earlier. That wasn't fair."
- **Reset:** "Whatever happened earlier is done with me. Are we good?"
- **Curiosity:** "I was annoyed earlier. Help me understand what you were trying to say."

You don't need to grovel. You don't need to win the argument. You just need to close the loop.

**Why it works:** Psychological safety research (Edmondson, 1999) shows that teams that repair after conflict have stronger trust than teams that never conflict. The repair, not the absence of conflict, is what builds the culture.

## Tool 4: The Newcomer Welcome

**When to use:** when a new player joins the squad.

**How to do it:** within the first 15 minutes of her first session:

1. Make eye contact and say your name first. Don't make her introduce herself cold.
2. Ask her one thing about her playing background. "What club are you in?" "What position do you play?"
3. Pair up with her in the first drill if possible.
4. Sit beside her at the next team meal.

That's it. You don't need to host her or take her under your wing. You just need to be the first person who treated her like a person.

**Why it works:** belonging research (Baumeister & Leary, 1995) shows the first impression of a group sets the tone for the rest of the relationship. Five minutes of intentional welcome can shape months of fit.

## Putting it together

Pick one tool this week. Don't try to do all four.

The highest-leverage one for most squads is **Tool 1: Specific Recognition**. It costs nothing, it lands well, and it shifts the culture quickly when more than one person starts doing it.

The hardest one is **Tool 2: The Assertive Request**, because it requires you to act when something is wrong. Most players let things slide for so long that the issue becomes resentment by the time they speak up. Practice in low-stakes moments first.
