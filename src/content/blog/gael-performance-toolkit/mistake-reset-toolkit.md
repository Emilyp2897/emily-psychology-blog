This is the Month 3 toolkit, paired with [Understanding Mistakes and Perfectionism](/content-hub/training-the-mind/understanding-mistakes-and-perfectionism/) and [Applying Mistake Resilience in LGFA](/content-hub/training-the-mind/applying-mistake-resilience-in-lgfa/).

One tool to use before play, four to use in-game, and one to use post-game.

## Tool 1: The 10-Second Reset

**When to use:** immediately after any in-game mistake.

**How to do it:**
1. Feel it for 3 seconds. Don't pretend it didn't happen.
2. Physical reset. Clap, shake your hands, roll your shoulders. Something visible to you.
3. Cue word. "Next ball." "Gone." "Reset." Same word every time.
4. Eyes up. Find the ball. Find your player. Find your role.

**Why it works:** the brain needs a clear "the moment is over" signal. The physical action plus the cue word together create that signal. Research on attentional control (Beilock & Carr, 2001) shows that external cues are more effective than self-talk alone.

**Bonus, use the cue word as a mantra:** the same word you reach for after a mistake is also useful WHILE you play. Pick one ("Next," "Calm," "Sharp," "Trust") and repeat it quietly between plays. It keeps your attention on the immediate job and pulls it back when it drifts.

## Tool 2: The "Fact, Lesson, Drop" Process

**When to use:** when a mistake won't leave your head between plays.

**How to do it (silently, in 5 seconds):**
1. **Fact:** what actually happened. "I played the wrong pass."
2. **Lesson:** what's the one piece of useful information. "She doesn't make that diagonal."
3. **Drop:** out loud or in your head. "Done."

**Why it works:** this is the mastery framing from error management research (Frese & Keith, 2015). You take the data and you close the loop. Without the lesson step, your brain keeps reopening the mistake. With it, the brain feels resolved.

## Tool 3: The "Verdict Catch"

**When to use:** when your inner voice starts saying something general about you as a player.

**How to do it:**
1. Notice the thought. "I'm useless." "I shouldn't be here." "I'm letting everyone down."
2. Name it: "that's the verdict, not the fact".
3. Replace it with a process question: "What's my next job?"

**Why it works:** Cognitive-Behavioral interventions in sport psychology show that the gap between event and reaction is where confidence damage happens. Catching the verdict before it becomes belief is one of the highest-leverage skills available to you.

## Tool 4: The Body Reset

**When to use:** when you can feel yourself tightening up after a mistake, OR any time mid-game when anxiety starts climbing and your head feels overloaded.

**How to do it:**
1. One long exhale (longer than the inhale).
2. Drop your shoulders. Notice if your jaw is clenched.
3. Wiggle your fingers in your gloves, or feel your feet inside your boots. Feel the gumshield in your mouth.
4. Narrow your focus to ONE thing. Your player. The ball. Your position. Nothing else.
5. Take one normal breath. Go.

**Why it works:** tension and overthinking feed each other. Loosening the body interrupts the loop. The long exhale specifically activates the parasympathetic system, which lowers heart rate and reduces fight-or-flight signals. Narrowing to one focal point pulls attention out of the head and back into the game.

## Tool 5: The Pre-Play 2-Breath Reset

**When to use:** before kickoff, before a free, after half-time. Any pause in play long enough to land yourself before the next action.

**How to do it:**
1. **Breath 1:** slow your body. Inhale through the nose for 4 counts, exhale slow.
2. **Breath 2:** lock in your focus. On the exhale, say your cue word in your head ("Next," "Sharp," whatever you've chosen).
3. Eyes up. First action ready.

**Why it works:** the first breath drops physical arousal; the second breath re-anchors attention. Two breaths is short enough to fit into any natural pause in play, long enough to break the spiral of pre-play overthinking.

## Tool 6: The Post-Match Process (do this within 24 hours)

**When to use:** after every match, win or lose.

**How to do it:**
1. Write 3 things you did well. Specific. Not "I played well". "I won my first 50-50."
2. Write 1 thing you'd do differently. Specific. Not "I played badly". "I'd start tighter on my marker for the first 5 minutes."
3. Stop. Don't keep writing. The point is to process, not ruminate.

**Why it works:** without an active processing step, mistakes either get buried (and stay there as unprocessed anxiety) or get rerun on a loop. Structured reflection moves them out of working memory and into learned experience.

## Putting it together

Before play (kickoff, after a stoppage, half-time):
- Pre-Play 2-Breath Reset to land yourself for the next action.

In a game:
- Mistake happens → 10-Second Reset (and use your cue word as a mantra between plays).
- Won't leave your head → Fact, Lesson, Drop.
- Inner voice starts attacking you → Verdict Catch.
- Body tightening up or anxiety climbing → Body Reset (narrow your focus to one thing).

After the game (within 24 hours):
- 3 wins, 1 improvement, stop.

If you build these into how you play, mistakes stop being a confidence threat. They become information.
