Month 8 toolkit. Paired with [Understanding Body Image in Sport](/content-hub/training-the-mind/understanding-body-image-in-sport/) and [Applying Body Confidence and Fuelling in LGFA](/content-hub/training-the-mind/applying-body-confidence-and-fuelling-in-lgfa/).

This toolkit deals with sensitive content. The fuelling guidance here is general. For tailored plans, work with a registered sports dietitian (not a wellness influencer).

## Tool 1: The Simple Fuelling Framework

**When to use:** as a baseline checklist for training days.

**How to do it:**

For training days, aim for:

- **2-3 hours before:** Carbs + protein. Something that sits well. Examples: porridge with banana and Greek yoghurt; chicken sandwich; pasta with chicken and veg.
- **30-60 minutes before:** Small carb snack if needed. Banana. Toast and honey. Sports drink.
- **During sessions over 60 minutes:** Sips of water or sports drink. Maybe a half-banana at half time for longer matches.
- **Within 60 minutes after:** Carbs + protein. The 30-minute "anabolic window" is overrated but eating within an hour helps recovery. Examples: chocolate milk, recovery shake, full meal if you can.
- **Across the day:** Don't skip breakfast. Eat real meals. Snacks between if needed.

**Why it works:** general sports nutrition principles apply. Female athletes specifically tend to under-fuel; this baseline corrects the most common gaps. For specifics, see a registered dietitian.

## Tool 2: The Language Audit

**When to use:** for one week. Notice the food language around you and in your own head.

**How to do it:**

Listen for:

- **Moralising:** "good" food, "bad" food, "treat", "cheat day", "earned it".
- **Body comments:** "you've leaned out", "you look in shape", "you've put on", "she's tiny".
- **Comparisons:** "I shouldn't eat as much as her", "she gets away with it".
- **Guilt language:** "I shouldn't have", "I'll regret this".

For each pattern you notice, just notice it. No need to call it out in the moment.

After a week, review. Where did most of the language come from? Coaches? Teammates? You? Family?

The goal isn't to police anyone. It's to see clearly what culture you're swimming in.

**Why it works:** food and body language shapes how athletes relate to fuel and self. Noticing it is the first step to changing it.

## Tool 3: The Body-Comment Boundary

**When to use:** when a teammate, coach, or family member comments on your body.

**How to do it:**

You have options.

- **Stay quiet, log it:** if it's not the moment, note it mentally. Pattern over time is what you respond to, not single comments.
- **Deflect:** "Thanks. How about that game on Sunday?" Move the conversation.
- **Address it later, privately:** "When you commented on my body last week, it affected me. I'd appreciate if you focused on how I'm playing instead."
- **For repeat offenders:** "I'm not going to talk about my body. Let's talk about something else."

You don't owe anyone an explanation for why their comment landed badly. You're allowed to set this boundary.

**Why it works:** body comments accumulate. If you let them all pass without comment, the culture stays the same. If you push back even occasionally, it shifts the norm.

## Tool 4: The Period Tracker

**When to use:** continuously. Track your cycle for season-long awareness.

**How to do it:**

Use any period tracking app, or a calendar.

Track:
- Day of cycle (Day 1 = first day of period).
- Symptoms (cramps, mood, energy, sleep).
- Training how you felt (heavy, normal, light, off).

Why bother:
- Awareness of where you are in your cycle helps interpret why training felt how it felt.
- Pattern recognition over months shows you what trains best where.
- Early warning sign if cycle becomes irregular or stops. That's a medical conversation.
- Lets you communicate with coaches/S&C about load if you choose to.

You don't have to share it with anyone. It's yours.

**Why it works:** menstrual cycle research (McNulty et al., 2020) is still emerging, but it's clear that cycle phase affects training response, recovery, mood, and injury risk for many (not all) athletes. Knowing where you are is information. Information is power.

## Tool 5: When to Seek Professional Help

**When to use:** if any of the following are true.

**Signs:**
- Your period is irregular or has stopped, and you're training hard.
- You're getting sick or injured more often than usual.
- You're afraid of certain foods, or have rules about what you "can" and "can't" eat.
- You're hiding eating or non-eating from others.
- Food, body, or exercise thoughts take up significant time in your day.
- A friend, family member, or teammate has expressed concern.

**Who to contact:**
- **Sports doctor / GP:** for medical assessment, period issues, RED-S concerns.
- **Registered sports dietitian:** for tailored fuelling plans. Look for someone who specialises in athletes.
- **Eating disorder clinician:** if patterns sound clinical (bodywhys.ie in Ireland; beateatingdisorders.org.uk in UK).
- **You can also email Emily directly:** emilyphelan@mindthegael.co.uk to talk it through.

**Why this matters:** the earlier you seek help, the easier the path. Athletes are particularly good at hiding food and body struggles because performance is celebrated. Don't wait until something is critical.
