Four tools.

## Tool 1: Process Goals (not Outcome Goals)

**When to use:** weekly planning.

**How to do it:**

For each week, write down ONE process goal. Not an outcome.

- Outcome: "Score 2 points in Sunday's game."
- Process: "Get into shooting position 4 times in Sunday's game."

- Outcome: "Be selected for the championship squad."
- Process: "Be the first to every ball in training this week."

- Outcome: "Lose 2kg by championship."
- Process: "Eat a protein-rich breakfast 5 days this week."

**Why it works:** Goal-Setting Theory (Locke & Latham, 2002) shows process goals are within your control. Outcomes depend on factors outside you (the opposition, the referee, the conditions). When you train your brain to measure success by process, you build evidence of progress regardless of outcomes.

## Tool 2: The 3 Bucket Goal Frame

**When to use:** start of a season, mid-season check-in.

**How to do it:** write three goals at three levels.

1. **Outcome bucket:** what would success look like at the end of the season? (Championship medal, county selection, breaking into the starting 15.)
2. **Process bucket:** what daily/weekly actions move you toward the outcome? (3 strength sessions a week, kicking practice every Tuesday, sleep 7+ hours.)
3. **Identity bucket:** who do you need to become for this to be sustainable? ("I'm someone who recovers properly", "I'm someone who speaks up in the squad", "I'm someone who plays free under pressure".)

Outcome goals motivate. Process goals build the work. Identity goals make it sustainable.

**Why it works:** layering goals across these three levels handles the failure modes of each. Outcome-only goals collapse if you don't get the result. Process-only goals can feel pointless without a why. Identity goals provide the durable foundation.

## Tool 3: The Micro-Goal for the Session

**When to use:** before every training session and every match.

**How to do it:** pick ONE specific thing you'll focus on. Write it down or say it out loud before the session.

Examples:
- "I'm working on getting back behind the ball within 5 seconds."
- "I'm working on calling my teammates by name this session."
- "I'm working on being the first to every loose ball."

After the session, check yourself against it. Did you do it? Did you forget halfway?

**Why it works:** large goals are too abstract to act on in the moment. A micro-goal is small enough to actually do. It gives every session a specific success measure that's in your control. Over time you build a pattern of consistent execution.

## Tool 4: The Energy Audit

**When to use:** when motivation feels low.

**How to do it:** answer four questions honestly.

1. **Autonomy:** Do I feel like I have choices in my training right now, or does everything feel imposed?
2. **Competence:** Can I see myself getting better, or am I plateaued?
3. **Relatedness:** Do I feel connected to the squad and my teammates, or isolated?
4. **Rest:** Am I getting enough sleep and recovery, or am I burning the candle at both ends?

The lowest score is where the motivation drain is. That's where the fix needs to happen, not in pushing harder on the same wheel.

**Why it works:** Self-Determination Theory shows motivation isn't a single thing. It's the result of needs being met. The audit makes the invisible drain visible.

## Putting it together

At the start of the season: do the 3 Bucket frame once.

Weekly: pick ONE process goal.

Daily: pick ONE micro-goal before training.

Monthly, or any time motivation dips: do the Energy Audit.

This is light-touch by design. Goal-setting that's too elaborate gets abandoned. Keep it simple, keep it consistent.
