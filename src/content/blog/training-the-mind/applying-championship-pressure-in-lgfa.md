[Last post](/content-hub/training-the-mind/understanding-pressure-and-fear-of-failure/) was about championship pressure and the fear of failure underneath it. This one walks through specific moments in the week leading up to a big match and what to do at each.

## Monday of championship week

You wake up and the match is in your head before you've even opened your eyes. That's how you know it's championship week.

What to do this week:
- Treat it as a normal training week. Don't add extra work. The fitness is already in.
- Eat properly. Hydrate. Sleep is the most important factor.
- Off social media if you can. If not, mute the noisy accounts.
- Have a low-stakes life conversation with someone. Not about the game. About anything else.

Don't:
- Watch hours of opposition video unless your coach is leading it.
- Start "extra" gym sessions.
- Get drawn into the WhatsApp tactical discussion at midnight.

## Wednesday or Thursday: a tough training session

Most squads have one harder session midweek. In championship week it can feel loaded. Like you're being tested.

The trap: a bad session becomes evidence of impending failure. "My touch was off, I'm going to play badly Sunday."

Reality: one session is one session. Your brain is just primed to read everything as significant in championship week.

The reframe: tough sessions are opportunities to build resilience, not previews of Sunday's performance.

## Friday: the night before pre-match

This is when sleep becomes a problem. You're tired. You should sleep. You can't sleep.

What helps:
- Have a wind-down routine you do every night, not just match nights.
- Phone away an hour before bed. Especially social media.
- Read something unrelated to sport.
- If you're awake at 2am ruminating, get up, drink water, sit in dim light for 10 minutes, go back. Don't lie there in the dark spinning.

Even if sleep is shorter than ideal, you can perform on it. The catastrophic thoughts about how bad you'll play because you didn't sleep enough do more damage than the actual sleep deficit.

## Match morning

Most players overthink the morning.

Simple structure:
- Wake at a similar time to your usual.
- Eat a real breakfast 3-4 hours before throw-in. Carbs + protein. Don't try anything new on game day.
- Light movement. A walk. Some mobility.
- Don't engage with the game in your head all morning. Let it be a normal morning.

The biggest mistake: spending the whole morning mentally rehearsing the game. That's hours of stress accumulating before you've even left the house. Save the mental engagement for the warm-up.

## Arriving at the pitch

You see the opposition. The crowd is starting to build. Your stomach tightens.

What to do:
- Pre-match routine, like any other game (see [Pre-Performance Routines](/content-hub/gael-performance-toolkit/pre-performance-routines-toolkit/)).
- Don't compare to the opposition. They're as nervous as you. Stay in your bubble.
- Find your senior players. Make eye contact. Steady each other.
- Don't try to make the speech of your career in the dressing room. The squad has been preparing for months. Now is execution.

## The first 5 minutes of the game

In championship games, the first 5 minutes are usually chaotic. Both teams are loaded with adrenaline. Mistakes happen.

What to do:
- Have a low-stakes opening action. Take an easy ball. Make a clean pass. Build a small win.
- Don't try anything ambitious until you've settled.
- If your first touch goes wrong, that's information about how nervous you are, not about how good you are. Reset.

The teams that get into the first 10 minutes without major errors usually win the appraisal battle. Their bodies move from threat to challenge state. The teams that make early big mistakes often spiral.

## At half-time

Half-time is its own moment.

Do:
- Hydrate. Eat if you need to.
- Take 60 seconds of quiet, not surrounded by people.
- Hear the tactical update. Take in two or three things, not twelve.
- Reset whatever didn't work in the first half.

Don't:
- Get drawn into a blame conversation if the first half went badly.
- Inflate yourself if the first half went well. The game isn't over.
- Add new tactical complexity in your head. Keep it simple.

## After the final whistle

If you won: feel it. You earned it.

If you lost: feel it. Don't pretend you're fine immediately.

For both: the post-match conversation in the dressing room shapes how the season's story gets told. If you won, name specific contributions, not just the obvious heroes. If you lost, hold the room together. Don't let blame start.

## What you can do this championship cycle

1. **Sleep routine all week.** Non-negotiable.
2. **Off social media in championship week.** Test it.
3. **One process anchor for the game.** Not "win". Something concrete.
4. **Audit your appraisal.** Are you in threat or challenge? Adjust where you can.
5. **Have a teammate ally** for both Saturday night and Sunday morning. Steadying.
