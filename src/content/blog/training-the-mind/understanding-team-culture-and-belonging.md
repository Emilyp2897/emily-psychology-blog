Heeyyyyy gaels, this is Emily.

Today is about team culture. A phrase that gets used a lot and often means very little. I want to give it real shape.

Team culture isn't motivational posters in the dressing room. It isn't the post-match singing. It isn't even how much craic the team has on the bus. Those are byproducts.

Team culture is how safe you feel saying something in the room.

## Psychological safety

The clearest research on this comes from Amy Edmondson (1999). She defined **psychological safety** as the shared belief that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking. In plain English: can I disagree with a teammate without being shut down. Can I admit I don't understand a tactic without looking stupid. Can I tell the coach I'm struggling without being labelled.

In teams with high psychological safety:
- People speak up about problems early
- Mistakes get talked about and learned from
- New players settle in faster
- Information flows up to coaches as well as down

In teams with low psychological safety:
- People go quiet
- Problems compound because nobody flags them
- Confidence is fragile because criticism is harsh
- Senior players hold all the social power

These aren't soft outcomes. They directly affect how the team performs.

## The Tuckman model

Bruce Tuckman (1965) described four stages most teams go through:

1. **Forming.** Everyone's polite. Roles are unclear. People are reading the room.
2. **Storming.** Conflict emerges. Disagreements about roles, tactics, effort.
3. **Norming.** The team works out its own rules. Implicit and explicit.
4. **Performing.** The team starts operating at its best. Trust is built. Roles are clear.

Most senior squads cycle through this every season. Especially after new managers, new players, or a tough end to the previous year.

Knowing what stage your team is in helps you not take everything personally. Storming feels awful when you're in it. It's also a sign the team is doing its job.

## Belonging

There's a separate but related piece of research on belonging. Baumeister and Leary (1995) called it a fundamental human need. We are wired to need to feel part of the group.

In a sports team, belonging shows up in small ways:
- Who you sit with at the team meal
- Whether anyone notices when you're late or absent
- Who texts you the day after a bad performance
- Whether your contribution gets named, not just the captain's

When belonging is low, you can be in the squad but feel outside it. That feeling drains energy. It also makes the small stresses (selection, mistakes, comparison) hit harder, because you don't have a sense of being held by the group when they happen.

## What kills culture

The research is consistent on what damages team culture:

- **Public humiliation** of any player by a coach or senior player. Even if it's framed as "tough love".
- **Inconsistent standards.** One rule for some, another for others.
- **Information hoarding.** Selection logic kept secret. Decisions made without explanation.
- **Cliques.** When the team has a social tier and you're not in it.
- **Treating mistakes as character flaws** instead of learning opportunities.

If you spot any of these in your squad, you're not imagining them. The research is solid on how much they hurt.

## What builds culture

- **Coaches and senior players modelling vulnerability.** Admitting they got something wrong. Asking for input.
- **Naming what's expected.** Standards for effort, communication, behaviour, written or said out loud.
- **Including the quiet ones.** Asking the bench player what they saw. Asking the new player what they think.
- **Reset-after-conflict rituals.** Disagreements happen. The repair is what builds trust.
- **Recognising specific contributions.** Not "great game everyone". "Sarah's three tackles in the second half changed the game".

## What you can do

You don't need to be captain to shape culture. Some of the most culture-shaping behaviours come from senior players who aren't in formal leadership.

1. **Notice the new player. Sit beside them once a week.**
2. **When a teammate makes a mistake, find them and say something.** Not platitudes. Something real.
3. **If something feels off in the team, name it to one person you trust.** Not the WhatsApp group.
4. **Recognise specific contributions out loud.** Not big speeches. Just specific.
