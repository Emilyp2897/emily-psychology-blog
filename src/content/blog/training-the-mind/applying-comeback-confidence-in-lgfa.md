[Last post](/content-hub/training-the-mind/understanding-identity-and-fear-after-injury/) was about what happens to identity and fear during injury. This post is about the specific Gaelic moments during the comeback. The ones that don't get talked about.

## Watching from the sideline

You're injured. You're at the match in a tracksuit. Your replacement is starting in your position. She plays well. The team wins.

You're meant to be happy for them. Part of you is. Part of you isn't.

Both feelings are normal. Pretending you only feel the happy one makes the other one worse. The honest version: "I'm happy we won AND I'm scared what this means for my spot." Both true. Both ok.

Watching from the sideline takes mental energy. Make sure you're actually invested in the team's performance, not just monitoring your replacement.

## The first session back

You're cleared. You're in pre-training. The first proper session is tonight.

What's going to happen:
- Your touch will be a bit off. Months without ball-work do that.
- Your decisions will be a half-second slow. Match sharpness is its own thing.
- You'll be more tired than you expect. Match fitness takes longer than rehab fitness.
- Your brain will catastrophise: "I'm worse than I was. Maybe I'll never get back."

None of this is true after one session. But your brain will offer the catastrophic interpretation as fact.

Catch it. "This is a first session back. It's data, not destiny."

## The first contact

If your injury was contact-related (knee, shoulder, collision), the first proper contact is a moment.

You won't fully commit. You'll hesitate at the half-second when you'd normally throw your body in. Your protective instinct is still on.

Don't try to power through with bravado. That's how second injuries happen. Instead:

- Build up the contact gradually. Light at first. Increase over sessions.
- Pair up with a trusted teammate for contact drills early on. Predictable contact is easier than chaotic contact.
- Notice the moment of hesitation without judging yourself for it. It's information about where you are in your return.

The fear melts as you build evidence that the body is holding up. Not from one big test. From repeated small ones.

## The first game

Your first competitive match back will feel weird.

Possible patterns:
- You overplay it safe, give the ball away too quickly because you don't want to be in 50-50s.
- You overcompensate by going too hard, trying to prove you're back, and your touch is worse for it.
- You play well for 20 minutes then hit a wall as match-fitness gives out.
- Your timing is off for the first 10 minutes then settles.

All of these are normal. The mistake is making the first game's pattern mean something permanent.

What works: have a low-key goal for the game. Not "play well". Not "score". Something like "stay in my defensive shape for 60 minutes" or "make one assertive call in the first 10 minutes".

## When your spot is no longer yours

Sometimes the player who replaced you keeps the spot. You come back fit, sharp, but you're on the bench.

This is one of the hardest things in sport. It's also where character gets built.

Two failure modes:
1. **Sulk.** Disengage. Train half-effort. Wait for her to fail.
2. **Sabotage.** Subtle negativity to teammates. Body language at training that drags the team down.

Both are tempting. Both make things worse for you.

What works:
- Train like you're starting. Be the best version of you for your own integrity, not for selection.
- Be a great squad player. Cheer the team. Help her if she asks.
- Keep building evidence in training. Selection is rarely permanent. Sharp consistent training brings you back into contention.
- Get clarity from the coach. Not a soft conversation. A real one. What do you need to do to get back in.

This is where SDT's autonomy matters. You can't control selection. You can control what you bring to every session. Anchor to what you control.

## What you can do

1. **Honest emotional reckoning.** Don't pretend you're fine if you're not.
2. **Stay in the squad.** Physically present, mentally invested.
3. **Build comeback evidence one session at a time.** First session is data, not destiny.
4. **Don't rush the body.** Especially with contact.
5. **Have a process anchor for the first game.** Not "play well". Something specific.
