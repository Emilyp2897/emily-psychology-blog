[Last post](/content-hub/training-the-mind/understanding-off-season-mental-health/) was about why the off-season is harder than people expect. This one is practical. How to actually structure the off-season as a Gaelic player.

## Week 1: rest, fully

The week after the season ends, do almost nothing physical.

- Sleep as much as your body wants.
- Eat what feels good without optimising for performance.
- See the people you barely saw during the season.
- Don't go to the gym.
- Don't worry about losing fitness. One week of total rest doesn't move the needle.

This is the only week of the year where you can fully switch off. Take it.

The temptation: "I should keep moving so I don't lose conditioning." This is the voice we've been working on all year. Tell it to sit down.

## Week 2: light movement

Reintroduce easy movement. Not training.

- Walks. Long ones if you like.
- Swimming.
- Light cycling.
- Yoga if it's your thing.

The point is to keep your body active without putting it through the demands of structured training. Most athletes feel better with daily light movement than with total inactivity, but the body still needs the recovery from the season.

No tracking. No optimising. Just move because moving feels good.

## Weeks 3-4: re-introduce structure

Start building a non-sport-centred routine.

- Sleep at consistent times.
- Eat at consistent times.
- Have a weekly rhythm: certain things on certain days.
- One social commitment per week with someone outside the squad.
- One thing that's just for you. Reading, a course, a creative project.

This is where you're building the version of your week that doesn't depend on sport. It's hard if you don't have practice doing it.

> A specific off-season you remember well. What did you do with it? Did it leave you better or worse going into pre-season?

## Weeks 5-6: optional light conditioning

If pre-season is more than 6 weeks away, you can start very light conditioning here. Not training. Conditioning.

- Easy aerobic work 2-3 times a week.
- Mobility and basic strength.
- No high-intensity. No max efforts. No technical work.

If you're 4 weeks or less from pre-season, skip this step and let pre-season do its job.

The mistake: starting heavy "pre-pre-season" work because you're worried about being fit for pre-season. This puts you in a year-round training cycle with no real recovery, which leads to burnout 2-3 years later.

## What to do with the social shift

The off-season is when the WhatsApp groups go quiet. The dressing-room banter is gone. You'll miss it.

What helps:
- One squad social event in the off-season. A meal. A trip. Just something to keep contact.
- Reconnection with non-squad friends. Some of these have been quietly missing you all season.
- Don't fill the social gap with party-mode every weekend. That's a different problem (and tends to leave you arriving at pre-season worse, not better).

## What to do with the identity surfacing

Some athletes find the off-season the moment they realise how much of them was "the player".

Signs:
- Restless in a way that doesn't ease.
- Not sure what to do with weeknights.
- Feeling like you're "wasting time" if you're not productive.
- Persistent low mood that doesn't lift.

The work here is gentle, not heroic. Try:
- One non-sport activity each week. Just one.
- One conversation with someone outside the squad. Just one.
- A few minutes of writing about how you're feeling. Just for you. Nobody needs to see it.

If it's heavy, talk to someone. GP, sport psychologist, or one of the support contacts on the [resources page](/resources).

## How to come back well

Pre-season starts and the squad is back together.

The athletes who come back best:
- Arrive having actually rested (not having spent 6 weeks underperforming alone).
- Are excited but not over-excited. Hungry to play, not desperate.
- Have something else in their life that gave the break meaning.
- Don't try to be the fittest on Day 1. They build into it.

The athletes who come back worst:
- Tried to "stay ahead" through the off-season and arrive depleted.
- Spent the whole break doing nothing and now their body is shocked by training.
- Are anxious instead of excited. Already feeling pressure for the season ahead.
- Try to make a statement in Week 1 of pre-season and pull something in Week 2.

## What you can do this off-season

1. **Week 1: rest completely.** Sleep, eat, social.
2. **Week 2: light movement.** Walks, swims, easy stuff.
3. **Weeks 3-4: re-introduce structure.** Build a non-sport-centred week.
4. **Reconnect with people outside the squad.**
5. **Have one thing that's purely for you.**
6. **Notice the 2-week mark.** If mood isn't lifting, talk to someone.
7. **Come back to pre-season gradually.** No heroics in Week 1.

## The full-circle takeaway

You've been on a 12-month journey through the mental side of sport. We started with pressure and finished with rest. That's the arc.

The most consistent athletes I know take recovery as seriously as they take training. The off-season is the biggest recovery window you'll have. Use it well.

Be kind to yourself in this break. The work is done. This is the part where you put yourself back together.
