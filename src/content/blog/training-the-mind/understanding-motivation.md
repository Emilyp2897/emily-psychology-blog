Heeyyyyy gaels, this is Emily.

This month is about motivation. Specifically, the kind of motivation that gets you to training in November when there's nothing on the line. Not the motivation that shows up in the week of a championship final.

If you've played sport long enough, you know the difference.

## The two types of motivation

The clearest research on this comes from Deci and Ryan's Self-Determination Theory (1985, 2000). They split motivation into two main categories.

**Intrinsic motivation** comes from inside. You play because you love the sport. You enjoy the work. You like who you are when you're training. The reward is the doing.

**Extrinsic motivation** comes from outside. You play for recognition, selection, a medal, parents' approval, what people will say. The reward is the outcome.

Most players have both. The question is the balance.

The research is clear: athletes high in intrinsic motivation:
- Stay in their sport longer
- Recover faster from setbacks
- Have better mental health outcomes
- Report higher enjoyment even during plateaus

Athletes high in extrinsic motivation (with low intrinsic):
- Quit when external rewards drop off
- Are more vulnerable to burnout
- Tie self-worth to results in a fragile way

This doesn't mean extrinsic motivation is bad. It's about balance.

## The three needs

SDT also says we have three basic psychological needs that, when met, fuel intrinsic motivation:

1. **Autonomy.** Feeling like you have a choice in what you're doing. Not being controlled or pushed.
2. **Competence.** Feeling like you're effective. You can see yourself getting better.
3. **Relatedness.** Feeling connected to others. Teammates, coaches, the sport itself.

When all three are met, motivation flows. When one is missing, motivation drops, even if you "should" be motivated.

## Why some days you don't want to go

The "I don't want to go to training" feeling isn't laziness. It's information.

It might mean:
- You haven't slept enough, and your body is asking for recovery.
- Your competence need is unmet (you've been training without seeing progress).
- Your autonomy need is unmet (everything feels imposed; you can't see your own choices in it).
- Your relatedness need is unmet (you don't feel connected to the squad right now).
- You're approaching burnout (more on this in a moment).

Most coaches react to low motivation by pushing harder. Push through. Show up anyway. Sometimes that's right. But sometimes the better response is to look at what need isn't being met.

## The burnout curve

Burnout research in athletes (Gustafsson and others have done good work on this) describes three stages:

1. **Reduced sense of accomplishment.** "I work hard but I'm not improving." Confidence dropping. Effort feeling pointless.
2. **Emotional and physical exhaustion.** You're tired in a way that doesn't lift with rest. Training feels like a slog.
3. **Sport devaluation.** "Why am I even doing this." Caring less. Disengaging.

Burnout doesn't happen because you're not tough enough. It happens when load is too high for too long with not enough autonomy or recovery. Female athletes carrying full lives outside sport are particularly susceptible.

If any of the three stages above are describing you for more than 2-3 weeks, it's worth taking seriously. Not "push through". Actually pause and look.

## What you can do

1. **Audit your three needs.** Autonomy, competence, relatedness. Which one is low right now?
2. **Find one thing in your training that's purely intrinsic.** What part do you genuinely enjoy? Protect it. Make sure it stays in your week.
3. **Notice when extrinsic rewards start replacing intrinsic ones.** If the only reason you're training is selection, that's a warning sign.
4. **Take rest seriously.** Recovery isn't lazy. It's what allows intrinsic motivation to come back.

## The most important takeaway

Motivation isn't a moral failing when it dips. It's a signal. Something in the system is off. The job is to find what.

The strongest athletes I know aren't the ones who never lose motivation. They're the ones who notice it early and adjust.
