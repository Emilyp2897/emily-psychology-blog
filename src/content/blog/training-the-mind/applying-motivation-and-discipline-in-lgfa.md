[Last post](/content-hub/training-the-mind/understanding-motivation/) was about what motivation is. This one is about what it looks like in the rhythm of a Gaelic season.

## The November problem

A Gaelic season is long. By the time you're deep into pre-season in cold dark sessions, motivation that was easy in March is much harder to find.

This is normal. The intrinsic-motivation reservoir gets depleted by repetition, weather, social pressure, and the lack of any near-term outcome to anchor to. Players who push through with willpower alone often burn out by spring.

What works better:
- **Reconnect to the why.** Why did you start playing this sport? Find one specific reason that still matters.
- **Build one micro-goal per session.** Not the season. Today's session.
- **Find one player you train with.** Even one strong relationship in the squad makes the cold sessions easier.

## When external motivation takes over

A common pattern in LGF: in the first few seasons you played for the love of it. Then you got into a strong squad, selection became competitive, and now you're playing for the spot, the recognition, the validation.

This isn't a failure. It's a shift in motivation type. But it makes you vulnerable. If selection goes against you, or your form dips, the only thing that was holding you in the sport is gone.

The fix isn't to stop caring about selection. It's to keep at least one intrinsic thread alive. The drill you love. The friend you train with. The way you feel after a hard session. Something that's yours, regardless of outcomes.

## Discipline vs self-punishment

A lot of athletes confuse the two. They look similar from the outside. Internally, they're very different.

**Discipline** is doing the work because you're committed to the long-term outcome AND you can hold yourself with respect when you fall short. You set the standard, you fall short, you adjust, you go again.

**Self-punishment** is doing the work because you're afraid of who you'll be if you don't. Missing a session feels like moral failure. You can't rest without guilt.

Self-punishment looks like discipline in the short term. In the long term it leads to burnout, injury, eating problems, or just falling out of love with the sport.

The test: if you fall short of your standard, how do you treat yourself? If the answer is "I make a plan and try again", that's discipline. If the answer is "I tell myself I'm useless and double down to make up for it", that's punishment.

## The fitness test you didn't pass

Every team has fitness benchmarks. Sometimes you don't hit them. Depending on the squad culture, that lands differently.

In a healthy system: you didn't hit the benchmark, the coaches identify what to work on, you work on it, you retest. It's information.

In an unhealthy system: not hitting the benchmark is treated as character failure. You stay in your own head about it for weeks. Selection feels at risk. You start training harder than your body can recover from.

If the second pattern is happening, the motivation issue isn't yours. It's the system you're in. Worth thinking about.

## Recovery as motivation work

One of the most underrated motivation tools is taking proper recovery. Not "lazy" days. Actual recovery: sleep, light movement, time away from the sport.

When recovery is treated as part of the work, motivation stays higher across the season. When recovery is treated as a guilty pleasure or laziness, you burn out.

This is especially relevant for female athletes, where research consistently shows under-recovery is common and the menstrual cycle adds load that often isn't accounted for in training programming.

## Burnout warning signs in LGF

Things to notice:
- The drive to training that used to feel fine now feels heavy.
- Touch and decisions feel slower than usual, not just in one session but across weeks.
- You're catching more colds or minor illnesses.
- Sleep is off.
- You're irritable with teammates and family in a way you weren't before.
- The thought of championship season fills you with dread rather than excitement.

Any one of these on its own can be normal. Two or three together for several weeks is worth taking seriously.

## What you can do

1. **Find your intrinsic thread.** What part of the sport do you love regardless of outcomes? Make sure it stays in your week.
2. **Audit the discipline / self-punishment line.** When you fall short, how do you treat yourself?
3. **Take recovery as seriously as training.** Plan it. Protect it.
4. **Notice the warning signs.** Two or three for more than two weeks: pause and look.
