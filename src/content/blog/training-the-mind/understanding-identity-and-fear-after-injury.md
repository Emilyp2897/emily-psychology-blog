Heeyyyyy gaels, this is Emily.

This month is about injury. Specifically the part that doesn't show up on a scan. The identity shift. The fear. The way you're meant to come back as if nothing happened, when something definitely happened.

A lot of athletes go through injury with brilliant physical care and almost no support for the mental side. Then we wonder why so many people don't come back the same.

## The athletic identity problem

Brewer, Van Raalte and Linder (1993) introduced **athletic identity**: how much of your self-concept is tied to being an athlete.

For most ladies Gaelic players, athletic identity is high. The sport is woven into your routine, your social life, who you are in your family, how you describe yourself. That's not a problem most of the time.

When you get injured, the high athletic identity becomes a problem. Suddenly the thing that anchors your sense of self is taken away. You don't just have a sore knee. You have a sore knee AND no idea who you are without the sport.

The research shows higher athletic identity is associated with worse mental health outcomes during injury. Not because high identity is bad, but because losing access to it without other supports is hard.

## The grief that nobody calls grief

There's a recognised pattern of psychological responses to athletic injury that maps closely to grief. Players go through:

1. **Denial.** "It's not as bad as they say. I'll be back next week."
2. **Anger.** At the body. At the timing. At whoever or whatever caused it.
3. **Bargaining.** "If I do extra rehab, I'll be back faster." "If I cross-train, I won't lose fitness."
4. **Depression.** Low mood, low motivation, withdrawal from the squad.
5. **Acceptance.** The injury is real, the timeline is what it is, recovery is the work now.

Not everyone goes through all of these in order. But the patterns are real. Pretending you're fine when you're in stage 4 doesn't help anyone.

## Fear of re-injury

This is the one that surprises athletes most. You did all the rehab. You're cleared to play. You step back on the pitch and your body is tight. Your reactions are half a second slow. You're protecting the joint without meaning to.

This is normal. The research has a name for it: **kinesiophobia** (fear of movement / re-injury). It affects up to 50% of athletes returning from significant injuries.

It's a protective mechanism. Your body learned that movement caused damage. It's now reluctant to fully commit. The fix isn't to push through with willpower. It's a graded re-exposure to the moments you're afraid of, with success at each level building trust back.

## The pressure to come back too fast

Female athletes returning from injury often face pressure (from coaches, from themselves, from social media comparisons to other players) to come back faster than is wise.

Bone, tendon, and ligament tissue heals on biological timeframes that don't care about championship dates. ACL reconstructions specifically have re-injury rates that stay elevated for 12+ months post-surgery.

Coming back too fast doesn't make you tougher. It makes you more likely to re-injure, which costs much more than waiting the extra weeks.

## What you can do

1. **Stay connected to the squad.** Even if you can't train. Sit in on team meetings. Stay in the WhatsApp group. The isolation makes everything worse.
2. **Have an identity outside the sport.** This is hard to build during injury. The athletes who get through it best had at least started it before.
3. **Be honest with yourself about the grief.** It's normal. Naming it helps.
4. **Don't push the return-to-play timeline.** Trust your physio and your S&C coach.
5. **Use graded exposure for fear of re-injury.** Don't try to ignore the fear. Walk through it step by step.

## The most important takeaway

The physical injury is half the work. The mental injury is the other half. Most athletes don't get coaching on the second part.

It's worth saying out loud: an injured athlete is allowed to grieve. To be angry. To be lost. To not have a positive attitude every day. The fact that you're an athlete doesn't take that away.

Coming back well means coming back as a whole person, not just a healed joint.
