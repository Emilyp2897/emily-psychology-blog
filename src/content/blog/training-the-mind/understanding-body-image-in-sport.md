This month I want to talk about something that gets danced around in most teams. Body image. The way we relate to our bodies as female athletes. The food language we use. And a real condition called RED-S that affects more players than people realise.

## The dual pressure female athletes carry

Female athletes get two opposite messages, often at the same time.

**From sport culture:** be lean, be fit, be strong. Your body is a performance tool. Train it, push it, fuel it for performance.

**From wider culture:** be thin, be small, look a certain way. Your body is judged on appearance, not function.

These two messages don't agree. Athletes get caught in the middle. The strong legs that drive your sprints are also the legs people make comments about. The visible musculature that lets you tackle is also "too much" in a dress.

Most male athletes don't carry this dual pressure to the same degree. For female athletes it's everywhere: in changing rooms, on social media, in passing comments from family, in the way clothes fit.

## Social Comparison Theory in body image

Festinger's Social Comparison Theory (1954) explains why we compare ourselves to others around us. In an athletic setting, that comparison is everywhere.

You compare to teammates in the changing room. You compare to elite players on Instagram. You compare to a past version of yourself in old photos.

The research (Tiggemann & Slater, 2013 and others) is clear: social media specifically, and image-based platforms especially, are associated with worse body image outcomes in young women. Even brief exposure to image-curated feeds can shift mood and body satisfaction within minutes.

This isn't a moral failing. It's a documented psychological effect. The fix isn't to "be more confident". The fix is to manage exposure and have stronger internal anchors.

## RED-S: the thing more female athletes have than know

**Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport** (RED-S) is a clinical condition that occurs when an athlete is in low energy availability for a sustained period. In plain English: you're burning more energy than you're taking in, and your body starts shutting down non-essential systems to cope.

The IOC consensus statement (Mountjoy et al., 2014, updated since) documents the consequences:

- Bone health declines. Stress fractures become more likely.
- Menstrual cycle changes or stops. Amenorrhea.
- Immune function drops. You get sick more often.
- Mood and motivation drop.
- Performance plateaus despite hard training.
- Sleep gets worse.
- Cardiovascular health declines.

RED-S affects athletes across body types. You don't have to be visibly underweight. Female athletes are particularly susceptible because of the dual cultural pressure to be lean.

This is not a "be tougher" issue. It's a fuelling issue with serious medical consequences. If any of those signs ring true for you, talk to a sports doctor or a registered dietitian.

## How fuelling actually works for female athletes

The research is consistent. Female athletes generally underfuel. Especially around training days.

What proper fuelling looks like, in broad strokes:

- **Before training/match:** carbs 2-3 hours before. Protein with it. Something that sits well in the stomach.
- **During longer sessions:** depending on duration, carbs in liquid or solid form.
- **After training/match:** carbs + protein within 60 minutes. This is when recovery happens.
- **Across the day:** consistent intake, not skipping meals.

Specifics depend on your body, your training load, your goals. A registered sports dietitian (not a wellness influencer) is the right person to give you a tailored plan.

## The language around food in teams

Listen to how food is talked about in your squad. You'll hear patterns.

Bad patterns:
- Foods labelled "good" or "bad".
- Players bragging about not eating before a session.
- Comments on what teammates are eating.
- "Earning" food by training hard.
- Treating dessert as guilt-inducing.

These patterns aren't innocent. They normalise disordered relationships with food, especially for younger players watching their seniors.

Good patterns:
- Food as fuel. Different fuels for different sessions.
- Talking about WHEN you eat as much as what.
- Players openly carrying snacks to training without shame.
- Eating with each other on team days.

## What you can do

1. **Audit your social media.** If specific accounts make you feel worse about your body, mute or unfollow. This isn't weak; it's protective.
2. **Notice the food language in your team and your own head.** Catch "good" and "bad" labels.
3. **Track your menstrual cycle.** If it's irregular or stopped, talk to a sports doctor. This is information, not failure.
4. **If you're showing RED-S signs, seek help.** Registered sports dietitian, sports doctor. Don't try to manage this alone.
5. **Eat for the training load you're doing.** Not for the body you wish you had.

## The most important takeaway

Your body is a performance tool. Treat it like one. The athletes who perform consistently across long seasons fuel well, sleep well, and have a stable enough relationship with their body to push hard without punishing it.

If your relationship with food, body image, or fuelling is affecting your wellbeing, that's worth professional support. There's no shame in it. A lot of athletes are quietly carrying it.
