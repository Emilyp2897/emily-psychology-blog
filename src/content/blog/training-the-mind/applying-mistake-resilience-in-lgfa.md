## The free you should have nailed

You've nailed this kind of free in training a hundred times. You step up, you strike, it drifts wide. Or worse, it's blocked down.

The mistake itself is two seconds. The damage gets done in the next 30 seconds.

What usually happens:
- You walk back to your position, head down
- You're replaying the strike in your head
- Your team has the ball again but you're still on the missed free
- The next play comes and you're not on it

What needs to happen:
- 5 seconds to feel it (don't pretend you don't)
- A physical reset (clap your hands, shake out your arms)
- A cue word ("next ball")
- Eyes back on the play

The whole reset is under 10 seconds. Anyone can do it. The hard part is doing it instead of carrying the mistake.

## The misread pass

You played a 20-yard ball expecting your teammate to be running into space. She'd already turned the other way. Turnover. Counter-attack. They score.

The thing about a misread pass is that you can't tell yourself "it wasn't my fault" because partly it was. You weighted the wrong cue. You misread her body language.

The reset here isn't denial. It's information processing.

Mistake: misread her run.
Lesson: she's not the type to make that diagonal run. Note it for next time.
Drop it: next ball.

This is what error management research calls "mastery framing". You take the data from the mistake and use it. You don't let it become a verdict on you as a player.

## The defensive mistake that leads to a score

You got beaten on the inside. They scored from it. Now you're 3 points down and you're seething at yourself.

This one is hardest because there's an immediate consequence visible to everyone. Your teammates saw it. The crowd saw it. The score reflects it.

The temptation is to overcompensate. Go harder, tackle harder, push higher. That often leads to a second mistake.

Better response: stay in your job. Same defensive shape, same positioning. The score is already on the board. Going chasing makes another one likely.

## The training session where everything was off

Some sessions, nothing clicks. Your touch is heavy. Your decisions are slow. You're a step late on everything.

Two responses are common.

Response 1: spiral. "I'm getting worse. The coaches are noticing. I'm losing my spot."

Response 2: shrug. "Today was rough. Tomorrow's a new session."

Response 2 is much harder than it sounds. It requires you to NOT use a bad session as evidence for a bigger story about your trajectory.

One bad session isn't a trajectory. It's a session.

## When your mistake costs the team

This is the one that haunts players longest. A mistake in a high-stakes game that visibly contributed to losing.

Some thoughts:

- Your teammates made other mistakes too. Other people's mistakes count the same as yours. Your brain is biased to weight YOUR mistakes heavier.
- A 60-minute game is decided by hundreds of decisions across 30 players. The idea that ONE moment lost it is almost always wrong, even when it feels true.
- The point of mistake research is that mistakes are how you get better. You can't get better without making them. The players who become great are the ones who can sit with the cost of mistakes without folding.

This doesn't make the feeling go away. But it puts it in perspective.

## What you can do this week

1. **Build a 10-second mistake reset.** Cue, breath, eyes up. Practice it in training when stakes are low.
2. **Catch the verdict.** When your brain tries to make a mistake mean something about you as a player, name it: "that's the verdict, not the fact".
3. **Process, don't replay.** Spend 60 seconds with the mistake (what happened, what you'd do differently). Then close the loop and move on.
4. **Track wins as much as losses.** Your mental ledger is biased. Help it.
