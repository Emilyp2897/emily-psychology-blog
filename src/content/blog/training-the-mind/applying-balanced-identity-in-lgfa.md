## The dual-career reality

Most ladies Gaelic players are not full-time athletes. You have a job. Or college. Or kids. Or all of it.

Wylleman and Lavallee's (2004) research on athletic career transitions explicitly maps this out: female athletes consistently report higher dual-career load than male athletes at equivalent levels, with less institutional support.

This isn't fair. It also doesn't change. What does change is how you carry it.

Two failure modes:

1. **Treating work/study as "second" to sport.** You half-show-up at the day job because sport is the priority. Your career stalls. You feel guilty about being mediocre at work. Stress accumulates.

2. **Treating sport as something you fit around work.** You skip recovery. You miss optional sessions. You drag yourself to training half-dead. Performance plateaus. You feel guilty about being mediocre at sport.

Neither works in the long run. What does work: be honest about which is in the foreground at any given period, and let the other be sustainably maintained rather than peak.

## The off-season identity test

Most athletes find the off-season harder than they expect.

The sport you've structured your week around for months is suddenly absent. The dressing room, the WhatsApp banter, the weekly purpose, gone for 6-8 weeks.

If your identity ratio is too sport-heavy, the off-season is when you feel it. Restless. Unsettled. Not knowing what to do with yourself.

Some signs:
- You can't sit still. Always need to be doing something.
- You overtrain even when your body is asking for rest.
- You spiral into "am I even an athlete anymore" thinking.
- You feel low for no obvious reason.

The fix isn't to fill the off-season with more sport. It's to use the off-season to feed the rest of your life. Travel, friends outside the squad, hobbies, work investment, family time. Things that get squeezed out during the season.

## Friendships outside the squad

The squad is going to be your closest people during the season. That's how it should be.

But if every single person in your phone is from the team, you have a problem.

What happens:
- A conflict in the squad and you have nowhere neutral to go.
- A bad season and the people you'd vent to are the people involved.
- Retirement comes and your whole social world goes with it.

What works:
- Maintain at least one or two friendships from outside Gaelic. Old school friends, work friends, hobby friends.
- These don't have to be deep daily contacts. Even monthly is enough.
- They're the ones who'll see you as more than the player.

## The career/work investment

A lot of female athletes treat their work or studies as "what I do alongside the real thing."

Two problems with this:

1. **You spend 35+ hours a week on something you've decided doesn't really count.** That's a lot of energy in something low-meaning.

2. **When the sport ends (and it will eventually, in some form), the career you've half-tended is what's left.**

The athletes I know who've transitioned best from playing days into their post-playing life are the ones who built genuine engagement with their career alongside the sport. Not full peak. Genuine engagement.

This doesn't mean sacrificing sport for work. It means giving work enough weight that it has its own meaning, not just being "the thing I do for money so I can train."

## When you're injured or out

When you can't physically train, identity questions get loud.

What helps:
- Stay connected to the squad even when you can't play. Sit in on meetings. Stay in the chat.
- Use the time to invest in other parts of your life. Career, study, relationships, hobbies.
- Don't try to fill the gap with EXTRA sport (e.g. obsessive rehab, watching every match). That feeds the identity over-attachment.
- Talk to someone if the identity loss is hitting hard. Not "I'm fine". Actually talk.

## What you can do

1. **Audit your identity ratio.** Estimate what % of how you think about yourself is "the Gaelic player". If it's over 70%, that's worth working on.
2. **Identify one non-sport thing you genuinely care about.** Career, hobby, relationships, a craft.
3. **Maintain one friendship outside the squad.** Even loosely.
4. **Use the off-season to feed the other parts of your life.** Don't just rest. Invest.
5. **If retirement / injury / non-selection feels existentially scary, that's a signal.** Not a reason to retire. A reason to broaden.
