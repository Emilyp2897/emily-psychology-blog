Heeyyyyy gaels, this is Emily.

This month we land on something nobody talks about enough: the off-season. The weeks or months between championship ending and pre-season starting up. Athletes describe these as "a break" or "downtime". For a lot of players, they're actually one of the hardest parts of the year.

I want to give you the research version of why, and what to actually do about it.

## What's happening in your brain and body

During the season, your week has a rhythm. Training nights, match days, recovery days, food planning, social structure. Your brain learns to expect this. Your body adapts to it.

When the season ends suddenly, all of that goes away at once.

What follows is a recognised pattern in sports research:

- **Adrenaline withdrawal.** Months of high-intensity training and high-stress matches have your nervous system running hot. When that input drops, you feel flat. Sometimes very flat. This isn't depression. It's withdrawal.
- **Sleep disruption.** Your sleep architecture has been shaped by training schedules. Without training, sleep often gets WORSE, not better, for a few weeks.
- **Mood drop.** Many athletes report low mood in the first 2-4 weeks of off-season. The combination of biological withdrawal and identity gap.
- **Restlessness.** Body is used to high load. Even with intentional rest, you can feel agitated.

This is normal. It's also temporary if you handle it well.

## The win-loss paradox

Winning the championship doesn't protect you from the off-season crash.

In some ways it makes it worse. The intensity of the championship win is followed by an even bigger gap. You're processing months of build-up plus the high of winning. The come-down can be steep.

Losing in the championship is its own version. Disappointment plus the gap.

Neither is "wrong" or "right". Both are recognised post-season patterns. The athletes who handle the off-season best don't fight the come-down. They expect it and work with it.

## Identity surfacing

Month 11 was about athletic identity. The off-season is when identity issues surface.

During the season, you're a Gaelic player. That's a clear identity, reinforced by your week. In the off-season, you're... what? You don't train. You're not in the squad WhatsApp as much. People aren't asking about the next match.

Athletes with high athletic identity (most ladies Gaelic players) often feel an identity drift in the off-season. Not because anything is wrong, but because the daily structure that held the identity together is gone.

This is uncomfortable. It's also useful information. The discomfort tells you something about how much of you is wrapped up in the sport.

## Loneliness and isolation

A lot of female athletes describe the off-season as lonely. Not in an obvious way, but in a way that creeps up.

You've been with the squad multiple times a week for months. Suddenly you're not. The casual contact, the shared challenges, the in-jokes, all of it drops sharply.

If most of your social life is the squad (Month 11 territory), the off-season is when you feel the cost of that. The friendships outside the squad you haven't tended start feeling further away.

## What good off-season mental health looks like

Some signs of a healthy off-season:

- A few days of decompression after the final game. Sleep more, eat what you want, sit on the couch.
- Then movement. Walks, swims, easy bike rides. Not training. Just movement.
- Reconnection with people outside the squad. Friends, family, old connections.
- One thing that's purely fun. A trip, a course, a hobby you've been ignoring.
- A reset of sleep and eating to a sustainable rhythm.
- Slow build-back into pre-season conditioning. Not heroics.

What an off-season should NOT be:

- Heavier training than during the season "to get ahead of the others".
- Total inactivity for the entire break.
- Pure social and party time with no rest.
- Filling every minute to avoid the come-down feeling.

## The 2-week marker

Most off-season mood patterns resolve within 2-3 weeks. If you're 3-4 weeks into the off-season and you're:

- Still struggling to sleep significantly.
- Persistently low in mood, not just flat.
- Withdrawing from people you usually like spending time with.
- Drinking more than you would have during the season.
- Feeling like life doesn't have much point.

That's worth taking seriously. Not "tough it out". Talk to your GP, a sport psychologist, or one of the support contacts on the [resources page](/resources).

The off-season can surface deeper patterns that the season's structure was holding together. That's not a failure. It's an opportunity to address something that's been there longer.

## What you can do

1. **Plan the off-season before it starts.** Have a structure, even a loose one.
2. **Take the first week off completely.** Sleep, eat, sit on the couch. Don't try to be productive.
3. **Then re-introduce movement and structure.** Walks, swims, easy stuff.
4. **Reconnect with at least one non-squad person per week.**
5. **Have one thing that's purely for you.** A trip, a hobby, a course.
6. **Notice the 2-week mark.** If things aren't lifting, talk to someone.

## The most important takeaway

The off-season isn't just a break. It's its own season, with its own challenges and its own work.

Athletes who treat it as "downtime" often come back to pre-season worse than they left. Athletes who treat it as recovery (real recovery, including mental and social recovery) come back better.

Be gentle with yourself in the off-season. The hard work is done. This is the part where you put yourself back together so the next season is possible.
