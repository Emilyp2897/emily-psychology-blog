Today we're talking about something every player has felt at some point… even if we don't always call it this.

That moment when you know you can do it… you've done it a hundred times in training… but in a match, under pressure, it just doesn't come out the same way.

We call this choking under pressure.

And I want to be really clear from the start. This is not about ability.

It's not that you suddenly become a worse player when it matters most.

It's about what happens to your mind in that moment.

## So what actually is "choking under pressure"?

In sport psychology, choking under pressure refers to a noticeable drop in performance in situations where the stakes feel high, even though the player has the ability to perform the skill.

What makes this different from general performance issues is that you can do the skill. You have done it before. But something changes in the moment it matters most.

Researchers like Baumeister (1984) were some of the first to explain this, showing that pressure can create a kind of "paradox effect". The more important the moment feels, the more likely performance is to break down.

## Why does this happen?

One of the strongest explanations comes from Beilock and Carr (2001) and later DeCaro et al. (2011).

They found that choking happens when performance shifts from being automatic to overly controlled.

So instead of trusting your body to do what it already knows how to do, you start consciously trying to control it.

In sport terms, that looks like:

- thinking about your technique mid-action
- overanalysing decisions
- becoming aware of movements you normally wouldn't think about

And that's where things start to break down.

Because skills in sport, especially fast ones like Gaelic football, are meant to be automatic.

## The "reinvestment" problem

Another key explanation comes from Masters (1992), who introduced the idea of reinvestment.

This is when under pressure, athletes start to "reinvest" conscious thought into skills that should normally run automatically.

So instead of: see ball → react

It becomes: see ball → think about technique → doubt → act

And that extra layer of thinking slows everything down.

This is where hesitation, tightness, and loss of fluency start to show up.

## Why some players "freeze"

Yu (2015) adds another layer by explaining that pressure doesn't just slow people down. It can overload the brain's control systems in high-stakes situations.

When that happens, attention gets pulled toward the importance of the outcome rather than the task itself.

So instead of "What is my next action?"

It becomes "I have to score this" / "I can't miss this"

And that shift is enough to disrupt execution.

## The key idea across all of this

Across all of this research, one thing stays consistent.

Choking is not a skill problem. It's a control problem.

It happens when:

- attention moves inward
- movements become over-controlled
- outcome becomes more important than process

And that combination disrupts automatic performance.

## How this is different from Attention Control Theory

This is where I want to make something really clear, because it connects to what I spoke about in my previous blog on [Attention Control Theory](/content-hub/training-the-mind/attention-control-theory/).

They are related, but they are not the same thing.

Attention Control Theory (Eysenck et al., 2007) explains what anxiety does to your attention in general.

It says:

- anxiety pulls attention toward distractions
- it weakens goal-directed focus
- it slows down efficiency (even if accuracy stays similar)

So ACT is mainly about where your attention goes under pressure.

Choking under pressure theories (Baumeister, Beilock, Masters, DeCaro, Yu) go one step further. They focus on what happens when performance itself breaks down in high-pressure moments.

And the key difference is this:

- ACT explains distraction and reduced efficiency
- Choking theories explain skill breakdown in high-stakes execution

So ACT is about attention being pulled away. Choking is about performance collapsing because control becomes too conscious.

## What it looks like in ladies Gaelic football

In a match, this might show up as:

- hesitating before a shot you normally take instinctively
- overthinking a free instead of trusting your strike
- tightening up in a 1v1 situation
- taking too long to release a pass
- feeling like your body is "not responding the way it should"

And again, this is not lack of ability.

It's pressure changing how the skill is controlled in the moment.

## The most important takeaway

Choking under pressure doesn't mean you can't perform.

It means your brain has shifted from trusting the skill to trying to control the skill.

And in sport, that shift is often the difference between playing freely and overthinking every action.
