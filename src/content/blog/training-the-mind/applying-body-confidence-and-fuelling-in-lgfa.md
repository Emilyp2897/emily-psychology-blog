## The changing room

The changing room is one of the most body-charged spaces in sport.

Everyone changing in close quarters. Different body types, different stages of life, different relationships with how they look. Some players have body confidence; others are doing everything they can not to be seen.

What I've noticed:

- Some players change facing the wall.
- Some players bring a long jumper to throw on the moment they're done.
- Some players take longer than necessary in the toilet to avoid the group changing window.

If any of these are you, you're not weird. You're carrying something. Worth thinking about whether it's a comfort thing, a body-image thing, or something deeper.

The most basic respect you can offer teammates: don't comment on bodies. Not even positively. "You've got really lean recently" can land badly even when it's meant well. Just leave bodies out of conversation.

## Training food

There's a lot of unsaid pressure around what you eat at training.

The senior player who shows up with a salad signals one thing. The senior player who shows up with a wrap and a banana signals another. New players read these cues, even if nobody intends them as cues.

The healthiest training-food culture is one where:

- Everyone fuels their training, openly.
- Food isn't moralised ("good food" vs "bad food").
- Different bodies need different things, and that's fine.
- Snacks during longer sessions are normalised.
- Nobody comments on what anyone else is eating.

If your squad has the opposite culture, that's a problem worth raising with a coach or senior, even if it feels awkward.

## The team meal

Team meals can be hard for players who are working through their relationship with food.

What happens at a team meal:
- You're eating in front of the squad.
- Portions might be different than what you'd eat alone.
- Other players might comment, joke, or compare.
- The pace is set by the group.

If team meals are hard for you, you don't have to suffer through them silently. Options:
- Talk to a coach or manager about portion size or menu options.
- Sit beside someone you trust.
- Have a plan: what will you eat, what will you leave, how will you handle comments.
- If meals are triggering a deeper pattern, talk to a registered sports dietitian.

## The comment from a coach or teammate

These land hard. Probably harder than the person saying them realised.

"You've leaned out, you look fit."
"You're in shape this year."
"Did you put on weight in the off-season?"
"You look thin."
"You're carrying a bit of weight, work on that."

Every one of these has consequences. Even positive-sounding ones. They put you in a body-evaluation frame that you then carry into training.

What you can do:
- If a comment hurts, say so. Not in the heat of the moment necessarily. Later. "When you said X, it affected me. I'd rather you commented on my play."
- Build your own internal anchor. The senior players who don't get derailed by comments have built their own measure of who they are as athletes that doesn't depend on appearance feedback.
- Find one trusted person to vent to. Don't carry comments alone.

## The missed period

If your period has changed (lighter, shorter, irregular) or stopped, this is information.

The casual response is: "great, no period to deal with."

The medical response: a missed period is one of the strongest signs of RED-S or related issues. It's associated with lower bone density, higher injury risk, and longer-term reproductive health concerns.

If your period has stopped or become irregular AND you're training hard, talk to a sports doctor. Don't sit on it.

## What you can do this season

1. **Stop commenting on bodies.** Yours, your teammates', your opponents'. Just don't.
2. **Fuel for the training you're doing.** Not the body you wish you had. Not what your skinny friend eats. What your body needs to recover.
3. **If your period is off, see a sports doctor.** Information, not failure.
4. **If team food culture is unhealthy, name it.** Quietly, to a senior or coach. Worth doing.
5. **Find a trusted person to talk to about body stuff.** Not the whole squad. One person.
6. **If you're struggling more than this, get professional support.** Sports dietitian, GP, or a clinician who specialises in eating concerns in athletes.
