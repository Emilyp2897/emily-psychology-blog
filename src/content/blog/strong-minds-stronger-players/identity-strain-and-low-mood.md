The athletes who care the most about sport often have the strongest athletic identity. The two go together. But strong athletic identity has a cost: when sport is taken away, even temporarily, the foundation can wobble in ways that are hard to predict from the outside.

Injury, end of season, retirement, deselection, a long break for life reasons. Any of these can trigger what researchers call identity strain, and it can look a lot like low mood. Knowing the difference matters because the response is different.

## What this can look like

- A flatness that arrives during off-season, post-injury, or after retirement.
- "Who am I if I am not a player" thoughts that will not go away.
- Loss of interest in the things that used to fill the gaps around sport.
- Withdrawal from teammates because you no longer feel like one of them.
- Reduced motivation, low energy, low mood that does not lift with rest.
- A sense that the future is unclear or pointless without the structure sport provided.

## When it crosses the line

Identity strain is a real and recognised phenomenon. Brewer, Van Raalte, and Linder's (1993) work on the Athletic Identity Measurement Scale shows that athletes with very high athletic identity are at higher risk for depression and adjustment difficulties when sport is disrupted.

The line into clinical territory is when low mood meets the criteria for depression: low mood OR loss of interest, plus other symptoms (sleep change, appetite change, fatigue, worthlessness, concentration difficulties), present most of the day, most days, for at least two weeks. Identity strain is a trigger for low mood, but low mood that has set in needs to be treated as low mood, regardless of what triggered it.

## What helps

- Acknowledge the loss. A transition out of sport, even temporary, is a real loss. Treating it as one is what allows it to move.
- Build a "second pillar". One or two interests, relationships, or pursuits outside sport that you can pour into. They do not need to replace sport; they need to exist alongside it.
- Stay connected to teammates even when you are not playing. Identity erodes faster in isolation.
- Reach out to former players who have been through the same transition. The GPA player development team specifically supports this.

## Where to get professional support

- **Your GP** is the first stop if low mood has been around for more than two weeks.
- **NHS Talking Therapies (UK)** for depression and adjustment-related work.
- **HSE Mental Health Services (Ireland)** via GP referral.
- **GPA Player Development** offers transition and post-playing support specifically for GAA athletes.
- **Switch the Play Foundation** (UK) at switchtheplay.com supports athletes through and after sport transitions.
- **Aware (Ireland)** at aware.ie for depression support.

## What I have learned

For years my identity was so tied up in being a player that any threat to playing felt like a threat to me. Slowly building things outside sport, and recognising the difference between "I am not playing this week" and "I am nothing this week," was some of the most important inner work I have done. The sport got better when I stopped needing it to be everything.

## Crisis resources

- **Samaritans** (UK and IE): 116 123 — 24/7 free listening line.
- **Pieta House** (IE): 1800 247 247 — suicide and self-harm support.
- If you are in immediate danger: **999 (UK)** or **112 (IE)**.
