Every athlete has had the experience of thinking too much. The shot you would normally take without thinking becomes a five-second negotiation in your head. The pass that used to come off automatically gets second-guessed mid-flight. That is overthinking, and the research is clear: trying harder to focus is usually what makes it worse.

But there is a point where overthinking becomes more than a focus issue. When it stops being a bad day or a bad month and starts feeling like a performance block, a yip, or a freeze that you cannot work your way out of. That is when it deserves outside support.

## What this can look like

- Movements you have done a thousand times suddenly feel unfamiliar.
- A hesitation in your body that you cannot explain or override.
- Specific situations trigger freezes (penalties, frees, one-on-ones).
- Persistent intrusive thoughts about a specific moment or movement.
- Dread before training because of what you might or might not be able to do.
- A pattern that has lasted weeks or months, not just one bad game.

## When it crosses the line

A normal overthinking phase clears with rest and a routine reset. A performance block does not. Beilock & Carr's (2001) research on Choking Under Pressure shows that conscious attention to automated movements is what breaks them down, a pattern called "reinvestment" (Masters 1992). The longer the block runs, the more anxiety reinforces it, and the harder it gets to interrupt on your own.

If you have been stuck in the same block for more than a few months, if it is affecting your sleep or your willingness to be at training, or if you find yourself avoiding specific scenarios (the free, the one-on-one, the start of a game), that is the point where a sport psychologist becomes the right call.

## What helps

- External focus. Research shows that focusing on the target or the outcome (the post, the player, the line) rather than the mechanics helps automated movements run.
- Cue words for refocus. A single word said in your head can interrupt the conscious-attention loop.
- Reduce the pressure during practice. The block runs because pressure is high; building the move back in lower-stakes contexts first lets the body relearn.
- Talk about it. Blocks often get worse in silence and better when said out loud to someone who understands.

## Where to get professional support

- **A sport psychologist** is the right professional for performance blocks specifically. BASES (UK) and PSI (Ireland) hold accredited practitioner registers.
- **Your GP** if the block is affecting your sleep, mood, or general anxiety.
- **NHS Talking Therapies (UK)** for the anxiety side of performance blocks.
- **HSE Mental Health Services (Ireland)** via GP referral.

## What I have learned

A block is not a character flaw. It is a brain that has learned to over-engage with a movement that needs to be left alone. The path back is not through more effort, it is through less. That is counterintuitive, and it is why outside help often makes the difference.

## Crisis resources

- **Samaritans** (UK and IE): 116 123 — 24/7 free listening line.
- **Pieta House** (IE): 1800 247 247 — suicide and self-harm support.
- If you are in immediate danger: **999 (UK)** or **112 (IE)**.
