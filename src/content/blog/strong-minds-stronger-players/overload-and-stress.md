Most athletes juggle. Training, work or study, family, social life, recovery, admin. There are weeks where it all fits and weeks where it does not. That is normal pressure, and most of the time you can rebalance over a few days.

What is different is when overload stops being a phase and becomes a state. When you are not just busy, you are constantly tipped over the edge. When stress is not just present, it is the background noise of every day. That kind of chronic overload changes how the body works, and it is worth taking seriously.

## What this can look like

- Constant tension in the shoulders, jaw, or chest that does not ease.
- Trouble sleeping despite being exhausted.
- Catching every cold, slow recovery from illness, frequent injuries.
- Short fuse with people you usually have patience for.
- Racing thoughts, especially at night.
- A sense of dread about Monday, or the start of a training week.
- Forgetting things, losing track, dropping commitments.

## When it crosses the line

Acute stress is short-term and the body recovers from it. Chronic stress means the stress response system stays activated for weeks or months at a time. Research by McEwen (1998) on allostatic load shows that long-term stress changes the body: cardiovascular system, immune function, sleep architecture, and mood regulation all suffer.

The clinical line is when stress meets the criteria for **adjustment disorder** or **generalised anxiety disorder**: persistent worry or distress, present most days, lasting more than a few weeks, and affecting your functioning at work, in sport, or in relationships.

## What helps

- Audit the load. Write down everything you are currently carrying. Some of it is genuinely fixed; some of it you can shift, delay, or drop. The audit usually shows more flex than you expect.
- Protect sleep. Sleep is what allows the stress response to switch off. Consistent bedtimes, no phone in the bedroom, limit caffeine after lunch.
- Move daily, even gently. Exercise is one of the most evidence-based interventions for stress.
- Tell someone what you are carrying. Stress shared is stress reduced.
- Say no to one thing this week. Even a small "no" gives the system a moment to settle.

## Where to get professional support

- **Your GP** is the first stop if stress is affecting sleep, mood, or physical health.
- **NHS Talking Therapies (UK)** for stress, anxiety, and CBT-based work.
- **HSE Mental Health Services (Ireland)** via GP referral.
- **Mind (UK)** at mind.org.uk for stress information and what helps.
- **Mental Health Ireland** at mentalhealthireland.ie.
- **Employee Assistance Programmes (EAPs)**: if you have one through work, it is usually free and confidential.

## What I have learned

I used to wear being stressed as a sign of being committed. It was not. It was a sign that I was carrying too much for too long, and that everything I was doing was suffering for it, including the sport. Cutting load was the most ambitious thing I did. The work that was left got better as a result.

## Crisis resources

- **Samaritans** (UK and IE): 116 123 — 24/7 free listening line.
- **Pieta House** (IE): 1800 247 247 — suicide and self-harm support.
- If you are in immediate danger: **999 (UK)** or **112 (IE)**.
