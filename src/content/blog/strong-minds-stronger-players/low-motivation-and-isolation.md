After a long season, most players want a break. Less structure, fewer alarms, fewer commitments. That is healthy, and a real off-season is part of how you keep playing for years.

But the off-season can also tip the other way. The lack of structure becomes lack of purpose. The space to rest becomes weeks of low motivation and isolation. The chat that came with training gets replaced by silence. By the time pre-season hits, some players are not rested, they are deflated, and the new year starts on the back foot.

That tip is worth knowing about, because the response is different from "I just need more rest."

## What this can look like

- Days slipping by with very little done.
- Loss of contact with teammates outside the immediate friendship group.
- Sleep going late and waking late, with no real rhythm.
- Increased reliance on alcohol or social media to fill time.
- A sense of flatness or "what is the point" that does not lift.
- Avoiding the off-season gym work because you cannot find the motivation, then feeling worse for skipping it.

## When it crosses the line

A few weeks of slower energy at the end of a long season is normal. The line into something more is when the slow energy stays, and starts coming with low mood, withdrawal from people, sleep disturbance, or a sense of pointlessness.

There is also a recognised pattern of **Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD)**, particularly in northern latitudes (Ireland and the UK both qualify), where shorter winter days trigger depressive episodes. If your off-season slumps land in winter months and repeat year on year, SAD is worth a conversation with your GP.

The general clinical line: low mood OR loss of interest, plus other symptoms, present most of the day, most days, for at least two weeks. If your off-season looks like that, what you need is not more rest, it is support.

## What helps

- Keep one anchor. A morning walk, a coffee with someone, a weekly gym session. The anchor protects rhythm.
- Schedule connection. Even one social commitment a week stops isolation from setting in.
- Daylight in the morning. Particularly important in winter; 20 minutes of outdoor daylight in the first hour after waking helps the body clock.
- Move daily. Walking counts. Movement is one of the strongest evidence-based protections against low mood (Schuck et al. 2016).
- Plan one thing to look forward to. It does not have to be big.

## Where to get professional support

- **Your GP** is the first stop if low mood or motivation has been around for more than two weeks.
- **NHS Talking Therapies (UK)** for depression, low motivation, and seasonal affective disorder.
- **HSE Mental Health Services (Ireland)** via GP referral.
- **Mind (UK)** at mind.org.uk for information on low motivation and SAD.
- **Aware (Ireland)** at aware.ie for depression support, including free online and in-person support groups.
- **GPA welfare officers** for GAA-specific off-season transition support.

## What I have learned

The off-seasons that hit me hardest were the ones that looked the most like "rest". No structure, no people, no movement. The off-seasons that worked were the ones where I treated rest as a different rhythm, not no rhythm. The structure was lighter, but it was there, and the people were still in my week. That kept me from sliding.

## Crisis resources

- **Samaritans** (UK and IE): 116 123 — 24/7 free listening line.
- **Pieta House** (IE): 1800 247 247 — suicide and self-harm support.
- If you are in immediate danger: **999 (UK)** or **112 (IE)**.
