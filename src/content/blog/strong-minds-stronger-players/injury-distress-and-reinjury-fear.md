When you are injured, the focus tends to land on the body. Rehab plan, return-to-play timeline, what you can and cannot lift this week. What gets less attention is what happens in your head while the body is healing, and what happens when you come back and discover the body is fine but the head is not.

Both can take longer to clear than the physical injury itself, and both deserve support.

## What this can look like

- Low mood, irritability, or tearfulness in the weeks after the injury.
- Persistent worry about reinjury that affects how you move on the pitch.
- Hesitation in the moment that used to be automatic (the tackle, the contest, the change of direction).
- Avoiding the situation where the injury happened, even after you are cleared.
- Loss of identity. "If I am not a footballer, who am I?"

## When it crosses the line

A short period of grief after an injury is normal. The injury has taken something from you and your brain processes that loss. The signs that something more is happening: low mood that lasts more than a few weeks, sleep disturbance, withdrawal from the sport and the people in it, a persistent fear of reinjury that does not reduce as you train more, or intrusive memories of the moment of injury (a marker for traumatic stress).

Research by Brewer (1994) shows that athletes with strong athletic identity are at higher risk of psychological distress after injury. The stronger the part of your identity that is the player, the harder the injury can hit.

## What helps

- Name the loss. An injury is a loss, and treating it like one (rather than just an obstacle) is what allows the grief to move.
- Stay connected to the team. Go to training even when you cannot participate. Isolation makes everything heavier.
- Graded exposure for reinjury fear. Slowly returning to the movements that scare you, in safe contexts first, is the evidence-based approach (Glazer 2009 on Tampa Scale of Kinesiophobia).
- Talk to your physio about the head side. Good physios understand that fear is part of rehab.
- Build identity outside sport. Even one or two things gives you somewhere to stand when sport is taken away.

## Where to get professional support

- **Your GP** is the first stop if injury distress is affecting mood or sleep.
- **NHS Talking Therapies (UK)** for processing trauma, anxiety, or low mood from injury.
- **HSE Mental Health Services (Ireland)** via GP referral.
- **A sport psychologist** can help with reinjury fear and return-to-play psychology. The BASES (UK) and PSI (Ireland) registers list accredited practitioners.
- **GPA** welfare officers understand the specific psychological side of injury in GAA sports.

## What I have learned

The hardest injury I had to recover from was not the physical one, it was the loss of who I thought I was while I could not play. Talking about that part, with someone who understood, was as important as the rehab itself.

## Crisis resources

- **Samaritans** (UK and IE): 116 123 — 24/7 free listening line.
- **Pieta House** (IE): 1800 247 247 — suicide and self-harm support.
- If you are in immediate danger: **999 (UK)** or **112 (IE)**.
